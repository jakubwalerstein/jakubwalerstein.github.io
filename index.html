        I've selected 5 songs that I think succinctly capture the range of my production style.

        1- 8888 features my friend Theo Kepler on vocals and myself on saxophone. This track is built around a layered synth and keyboard loop, which uses stacked fifths to create wide voicings of suspended and min7/9 chords.

        2- Oblivion will serve as the interlude of an upcoming project of mine. The dense harmonies on this track serve to create a hypnotic and uneasy vibe.

        3- Redhead was a ton of fun to make; I ended up playing saxophone, guitar, bass, and piano on this song (as well as singing). The percussion is built around sounds from the "Brownsburg Barn" sample pack displayed below.

        4- Many video games include a hub world, a neutral area free from enemies that allows the player to learn the controls in peace. I wrote Hub World as a score for the hub of a game being developed by a game design major at Purdue. It features some processed and reversed guitar samples that I recorded with my friend Sanjeev Rao.

        5- Despite its PBR&B influences, Sweet // 1991 is an ode to Appalachia. I recorded the vocal refrain into my laptop microphone in the backseat of my brother's car, having been inspired by the aging Pennsylvania coal towns we had been passing. I have tried many times since to re-record the refrain with proper equipment, but I could never capture the emotion of that first take. So, instead of fighting it, I decided to embrace the original recording's lo-fi quality and build this song around it. The song also features a 4-voice saxophone arrangement that I wrote to constrast the melodic shape of the vocal refrain.

    • Worthless Buddha Head (2018)
        Click to show/hide album notes:
          Worthless Buddha Head

          When I was in 7th grade, my older brother let me borrow his copy of The Notorious B.I.G.'s 'Life After Death' on CD, in exchange for a vow that I wouldn't tell our mom about it. I appreciated music as a kid, but something about this album captivated me in a way nothing ever had before.

          This was the first musical exploration I had done under my own direction. So, the topics of Biggie's verses, and his references to guns and drugs, all seemed to go right over my head. Really, what captured my attention so firmly was Biggie's sense of rhythm, his inimitable cadence and flow. A telling piece of trivia is that Biggie had a close relationship with jazz saxophonist Donald Harrison growing up, with Harrison serving as a musical mentor and even training him in jazz vocal techniques. To me, Harrison's influence on Biggie's delivery is unmistakable; no other rapper from the era held a candle to his mastery of syncopation, phrasing, and tone. Fittingly, my first impression of Biggie's voice was as an instrument, his verses as solos over pillowy late '90's production. Listening through this lens was absolutely hypnotic, equal parts nuanced and energetic.

          On Worthless Buddha Head, I try to place myself back in this mode of listening by treating Biggie as a soloist, letting his rhythmic choices guide the instrumentation I build around his verses. Real and Good Fella both pull from jazz and experimental electronic music, melding thick chord progressions with driving rhythms and psychedelic pads. The tracks Oshkosh (Interlude) and Oshkosh are based on processed piano samples from a VHS tape, ending the project on an organic note. These influences all come together with the goal of recreating the feeling of my formative encounter with Biggie's music a decade ago.
